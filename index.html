Origin Band

  • Course: 2.009 Product Engineering Processes
  • Date: Fall 2014

Origin Band is a tracking device designed for skiers to track their friends' locations without the need for wifi or cell tower networks. It was developed by my team (24 members) as part of the 2.009 Product Engineering Processes course in fall 2014. The theme of the class was Adventure.

The band analyzes GPS signals and utilizes radio wave transmission to communicate with other bands nearby. The users sync their bands before skiing, and the bands display real time location data on an easy to read interface.

Origin band was constructed from scratch using custom designed electronics, 3D printed casing and manual finishing and detailing. The project involved mechanical, electrical and software engineering modules.

I was the team leader of modeling & assembly, which oversaw CAD design, prototyping, specification, material sourcing and assembly.

The original idea of such a tracking device was targeted towards skydivers to help avoid collisions. After more market survey and logistical analysis, our team settled on skiers as the main target user. However, the device can be applicable to users in crowds, festivals or hiking as well.

Design Process


I created the first 3D concept model of Origin via Solidworks, which utilizes an altitude and direction display (eventually limited only to direction and distance). The concept model was imagined to be a sleek, thin and form fitting band with aluminum components.

Over the course of the semester, our team made more than 6 iterations of the watch design. We investigated many types of strap design, including continuous rubber casting and detachable elastic straps. Our final iteration settled on an elastic strap design that attached to the main band interface.

I led the effort to create the offical CAD assembly model for our assembly milestone prior to the final design. The assembly involved creating a blowout model of the watch and electronics.

During the process of creating the assembly, our assembly team also encountered several challenges. We had to figure out every single component to the assembly, the dimensions of the components, and the proper order. In addition, we needed to make sure all the internal parts were designed so that everything could fit inside and worked properly. From this experience, I gained valuable practice in leadership and making decisions in the face of uncertainty.

Operation Robot

  • Class: 2.007 Design & Manufacturing
  • Date: Spring 2013

The theme of the course was "operation", like the board game except using remote control robots to play (lift bones, pick up wrenches, retrieve butterflies, etc). My robot was designed to pick up bones and place them on shelves, which scored points based on how high you could lift them and how many you can do within a minute time frame.

I designed the robot in Solidworks and then waterjetted to create the parts. It utilized two servo motors to control both the up and down motion as well as the opening of the claw. I added some rubber padding and rubber bands to the grips and wheels seen in the pictures to test grip and traction. It ended up scoring pretty well in the competition!

Santa Phone System

  • Class: ESD.051 Engineering, Innovation, and Design
  • Date: Fall 2013

Santa's Residence is a phone system designed as a final project for ESD.051, Engineering, Innovation & Design Fall 2013. The purpose of the system is to help parents find out their child's wishlist for Christmas without the child user ever doubting the existence of Santa. It does so by first allowing parents to access a secret menu to set up the system, in which they can also retrieve the responses spoken by their child after using the system.

The system is designed so that once the parent sets up the phone system, their next call automatically begins a conversational exchange for their child with Santa. Santa engages in cheerful dialogue with the child, during which he asks for his or her top three gift wishes for Christmas. There is a database of recognized responses, for which Santa can reply back with confirmation of the detected gift. With unrecognizable or undetected responses, the system records and saves an audio clip of the child's response. After the call is finished, the parent dials back to the phone system's secret menu accessible by password and can retrieve the gift wishes of their child.

The project incoporated a tremendous amount of detailed design and back end programming, as well as user testing. The clickable pdf of the written report above delves into all of the details of the project and how the phone system works.

Line Tracker Robot

  • Class: 2.678 Electronics For Mechanical Engineers
  • Date: Fall 2012

I built and programmed this robot for our final project autonomous robot competition. The obstacle course involved varying patterns of black tape, and the robot utilized a photosensor at the front of the car to detect the changes in light saturation under the sensor. The movement of the robot was programmed in Arduino, for which I designed an algorithm that adjusted the robot to meet a certain light saturation on its sensor to help it turn and find the correct path to take.


Go Forth Project

  • Class: 2.671 Measurement & Instrumentation
  • Date: Spring 2014

The final project for this course was to design and conduct my own quantitative measurement project. I chose to analyze the dynamics of olympic weightlifting since it is my main athletic hobby.

My study used force plates positioned under my feet and several accelerometers to determine acceleration of an 80kg barbell and my shoulders, as well as peak normal force into the ground. The data was used to compute power generation during the sequence of the lift, and was used to compare the motions of the two stages of the clean: the 1st pull and 2nd pull.

The deliverables of the project included a full sized poster (viewable above as a pdf) and a term report.

IVC Filter Design

  • Class: Personal Project
  • Date: 2012 - Present, patent filed Fall 2013

An inferior vena cava (IVC) filter is a medical device designed to sit in the IVC to catch blood clots originating from deep vein thrombosis.

I created my own design as part of a personal project out of interest in medical devices. Through research, I found that current IVC filters had many flaws, including improper alignment, tendency to break and puncture, and difficulty during removal procedures from the body.

The design I created to solve some of the problems with current filters allowed me to file for a sole patent application late last year. I am currently in the process of developing the project in collaboration with a medical company in the testing phase of the device. The picture above is an early laser cut alpha prototype created from a single tube of surgical stainless steel. The cover photo is an image from the patent application.

Ice Bucket Challenge Trebuchet

  • Class: Side Project
  • Date: Fall 2014

The "ALS Ice Bucket Challenge" Trebuchet was a spontaneous project designed and built for MIT's president, Rafael Reif, who challenged MIT engineers to build contraptions to douse him with ice water with 24 hours notice. I helped my fraternity build this catapult and carry it from Boston to Cambridge via the Harvard Bridge.

Our trebuchet was one of only two contraptions built for the challenge. I am seen in the pictures loading up the buckets with the proper amount of ice water. There was also a GoPro video taken that captured the festivities.
